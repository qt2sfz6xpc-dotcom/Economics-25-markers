Example 25 Marker 1: Indirect Tax on Demerit Goods

Question:
Evaluate the effectiveness of indirect taxation on demerit goods such as alcohol and tobacco to reduce negative externalities.

Introduction

A negative externality occurs when the consumption or production of a good creates external costs for third parties. Demerit goods such as alcohol and tobacco are overconsumed because consumers often ignore or underestimate their harmful effects. The objective of an indirect tax is to increase the private cost of consumption and production, reduce quantity consumed, and move the market closer to the socially optimal level of output.

Point 1: Impact on Production

One effect of an indirect tax is that it increases firms’ costs of production. In the tobacco and alcohol industries, producers have to pay a tax to the government for each unit sold, which reduces their available finance for production. This causes supply to shift left from S1 to S2, increasing price from P1 to P2 and reducing quantity from Q1 to Q2. As production falls, fewer harmful goods are supplied in the market, reducing the negative externalities associated with alcohol-related illness, smoking-related disease and pressure on healthcare services. Therefore, indirect taxation can improve resource allocation by reducing the overproduction of demerit goods.

Evaluation 1

However, the effectiveness of indirect taxation depends on the price elasticity of demand. Goods such as cigarettes and alcohol often have inelastic demand because they are addictive and have few close substitutes. This means producers can pass most of the tax burden onto consumers through higher prices, while quantity demanded falls only slightly. As a result, the tax may raise government revenue but fail to significantly reduce consumption or negative externalities. There is also a risk of government failure if high taxes encourage black markets, smuggling or the sale of unsafe goods.

Point 2: Impact on Consumption

Indirect taxation may also reduce consumption by increasing prices for consumers. In the tobacco industry, higher cigarette prices may create a financial incentive for consumers to quit smoking or switch towards less harmful alternatives such as e-cigarettes. If the higher price reduces consumption, then the marginal private benefit of consuming the good becomes closer to the marginal social benefit. This reduces the welfare loss caused by overconsumption. Therefore, indirect tax can help correct market failure by making consumers face more of the true social cost of their actions.

Evaluation 2

However, many consumers of demerit goods behave irrationally due to addiction, imperfect information and short-termism. Even if prices rise, consumers may continue to purchase tobacco or alcohol because they prioritise immediate satisfaction over long-term health costs. Indirect taxes are also regressive, meaning they place a larger burden on low-income households. Therefore, although taxation may reduce consumption for some consumers, it may also worsen inequality and fail to fully correct the market failure.

Conclusion

Overall, indirect taxation can be effective in reducing negative externalities, especially because it affects both producers and consumers while raising tax revenue that can be used to fund healthcare or education campaigns. However, its effectiveness is limited when demand is highly price inelastic, as consumers may continue buying the good despite higher prices. Therefore, indirect taxation is most effective when combined with other policies such as regulation, education and restrictions on advertising.

Example 25 Marker 2: Subsidies for Electric Vehicles

Question:
Evaluate the microeconomic effects of subsidies paid to electric vehicle manufacturers.

Introduction

A subsidy is a grant given by the government to producers to lower their costs of production and encourage greater output. Electric vehicles may create positive externalities because they reduce carbon emissions compared with petrol and diesel cars. The objective of subsidising EV manufacturers is to increase supply, lower prices and encourage consumers to switch towards cleaner transport.

Point 1: Impact on Producers

One microeconomic effect of a subsidy is that it allows producers to expand production. In the electric vehicle industry, a subsidy could help firms such as General Motors invest in new factories, purchase capital equipment, retrain workers and fund research into battery technology. This reduces costs of production and shifts supply right from S1 to S2. As a result, price falls from P1 to P2 and output rises from Q1 to Q2. Therefore, subsidies benefit producers by increasing their capacity to produce EVs and helping them compete against petrol and diesel car manufacturers.

Evaluation 1

However, subsidies have a significant opportunity cost. Government funds used to support EV manufacturers could have been spent on healthcare, education or public transport. This is especially important because electric vehicles are not necessarily essential goods, and many firms in the EV market already have a profit incentive to innovate. As a result, subsidies may represent an inefficient use of taxpayer money if firms would have expanded production without government support.

Point 2: Impact on Consumers

Subsidies can also benefit consumers by making electric vehicles more affordable. As supply increases and prices fall, consumers who previously found EVs too expensive may now be more willing to buy them. This is important because petrol and diesel vehicles are becoming more costly due to fuel prices, congestion charges and environmental restrictions. Therefore, lower EV prices may increase demand for cleaner vehicles and reduce the market failure associated with the overconsumption of high-emission transport.

Evaluation 2

However, lower prices may not be enough to persuade consumers to switch. EV technology is still viewed by some consumers as unreliable due to concerns about charging times, battery replacement costs and limited charging infrastructure. If consumers prefer petrol or diesel vehicles, demand for EVs may remain relatively inelastic. Therefore, subsidies may increase supply without creating a large increase in actual consumption, limiting the policy’s effectiveness.

Conclusion

Overall, subsidies for electric vehicle manufacturers are likely to have positive microeconomic effects because they increase supply, reduce prices and encourage cleaner consumption. This is especially useful in the car industry, where petrol and diesel vehicles create negative externalities. However, the policy’s success depends on consumer confidence, charging infrastructure and whether the opportunity cost of subsidies is justified. Therefore, subsidies are effective, but they work best when combined with policies that make petrol and diesel cars less attractive.

Example 25 Marker 3: Monopoly and Collusion

Question:
Evaluate whether collusion and monopoly would always disadvantage consumers.

Introduction

A monopoly exists when a firm has significant market power, often defined as having at least 25% market share. Collusion occurs when firms cooperate, either formally or informally, to restrict competition, often through price fixing. In markets such as pharmaceuticals, monopoly power and collusion may disadvantage consumers because demand is often price inelastic and consumers have few alternatives.

Point 1: High Prices

Monopolies and collusive oligopolies may disadvantage consumers by charging higher prices. In the pharmaceutical industry, many medicines are necessity goods with inelastic demand because patients need them regardless of price. A monopoly can restrict output and set price above marginal cost in order to maximise profit where MR = MC. This leads to a higher price and lower output compared with a competitive market. Similarly, collusive firms may agree to fix prices artificially high. As a result, consumer surplus falls and consumers are disadvantaged through reduced affordability.

Evaluation 1

However, monopolies may not always charge higher prices if they benefit from economies of scale. Large firms can reduce average costs through bulk buying, technical economies of scale and research economies of scale. If these lower costs are passed on to consumers, prices may be lower than in a more competitive market. In the pharmaceutical industry, the NHS may also act as a monopsony buyer, using its buying power to negotiate lower prices. Therefore, monopoly power does not always disadvantage consumers if regulation or buyer power limits excessive pricing.

Point 2: Innovation and Quality

Monopolies and oligopolies may also benefit consumers through innovation. Pharmaceutical companies require large profits to fund research and development into new medicines. If firms earn supernormal profits, they can reinvest into developing new treatments, improving quality and increasing consumer welfare in the long run. This supports Adam Smith’s idea that the profit motive can encourage firms to produce goods that benefit society. Therefore, monopoly power may benefit consumers if it leads to dynamic efficiency.

Evaluation 2

However, there is no guarantee that supernormal profits will be reinvested. Large firms may instead distribute profits to shareholders as dividends or use them to strengthen market power. In pharmaceuticals, criticism of high medicine prices suggests that firms may prioritise profit over accessibility. If innovation is limited and prices remain high, then monopoly power reduces consumer welfare rather than improving it. Therefore, the argument that monopoly benefits consumers depends heavily on whether profits are genuinely used for research and development.

Conclusion

Overall, collusion and monopoly are likely to disadvantage consumers when firms use market power to raise prices, restrict output and reduce consumer surplus. This is especially harmful in the pharmaceutical industry, where medicines are necessity goods and demand is price inelastic. However, monopoly power may benefit consumers if economies of scale reduce prices or if supernormal profits fund innovation. Therefore, monopoly does not always disadvantage consumers, but without strong regulation it is likely to reduce consumer welfare.
